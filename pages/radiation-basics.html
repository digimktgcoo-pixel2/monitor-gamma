Radiation Basics

Understanding background radiation
Background radiation is a natural part of life on Earth. Every person on the planet is exposed to it every day from rocks, soil, cosmic rays, and even food.

Types of Radiation

Gamma Radiation

High-energy electromagnetic radiation that travels through the air and can penetrate most materials. This is what EPA RadNet monitors. Sources include cosmic rays, naturally occurring radioactive materials in soil and rock, and nuclear events.

Alpha Radiation

Heavy, slow particles that can't penetrate skin. Only dangerous if inhaled or ingested. Not measured by RadNet's air monitors.

Beta Radiation

Faster particles that can penetrate skin slightly. Can be hazardous with prolonged exposure. Some RadNet stations measure beta radiation separately.

What is a Sievert?

The sievert (Sv) measures the biological effect of radiation on humans. The readings on this dashboard are in nanosieverts per hour (nSv/h) — one billionth of a sievert per hour.

Annual Dose Context

The average American receives about 6.2 mSv per year from all natural and artificial sources combined. Background radiation from the environment accounts for roughly half of that. A single chest X-ray is about 0.1 mSv.
