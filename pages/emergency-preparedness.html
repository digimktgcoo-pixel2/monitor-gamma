Emergency Preparedness

What to know before, during, and after a radiation event
This page provides general public preparedness information only. In any actual emergency, follow instructions from local authorities, FEMA, and the NRC. Do not rely solely on this site.

Know Your Risk

Most Americans will never experience a significant radiation event. The most likely scenarios are limited industrial accidents or localized nuclear plant incidents — not nuclear war. Understanding the difference between types of events helps you prepare proportionally.

Before an Emergency

Build a Basic Emergency Kit

  • Water: 1 gallon per person per day, 3-day minimum supply
  • Non-perishable food for at least 3 days
  • Battery-powered or hand-crank NOAA weather radio
  • Flashlight and extra batteries
  • First aid kit
  • Prescription medications — 30-day supply if possible
  • Cash in small bills
  • Copies of important documents in a waterproof bag
  • N95 masks (also useful for radiation particulate events)

Know Your Local Plans

Find out if you live within 10 miles of a nuclear power plant. If so, your local emergency management agency has a specific Emergency Planning Zone (EPZ) plan. Contact them to understand evacuation routes and shelter-in-place guidance.

During a Radiation Event

Get Inside

The most important protective action is getting inside a solid building. Brick, concrete, and underground locations provide the most shielding. A basement reduces exposure by approximately 10x compared to being outside.

Stay Inside

Close all windows, doors, and fireplace dampers. Turn off fans, air conditioning, and heating systems that draw in outside air. Move to an inner room away from windows.

Stay Tuned

Monitor official sources: local radio/TV, FEMA Wireless Emergency Alerts, and NOAA weather radio. Do not leave shelter until authorities say it is safe.

Potassium Iodide (KI)

KI tablets protect the thyroid gland from radioactive iodine specifically. They do NOT protect against all radiation. Only take KI if directed by public health officials. Pre-purchasing without guidance is not recommended for most people.
